## Characters That Stuck

**Robert Sklyarov** ‚Äî The emotional center. A physically strong, intellectually mediocre lab technician who can't keep up with the theorists. Loves Tanya. His self-awareness about being "a museum-quality blockhead" is painfully honest. He's the one who manually drives a "kharibda" (wave-absorber) into the path of the Wave to buy time for others to escape. The "dumb" guy does the bravest thing.

**Gorbosvky** ‚Äî The starship captain. Gentle, lanky, carries a music player everywhere. He's the one who has to make the final call: children on the ship, everything else stays. The scene where he walks through the crowd toward the ship, accepting paintings, scientific papers, letters ‚Äî people handing him the things they want to survive them ‚Äî is devastating.

**Kamill** ‚Äî The most interesting for me. One of the "Devil's Dozen" ‚Äî experimental post-humans. He can die and resurrect. He can predict events. But the experiment "failed": instead of "want to but can't," he got "can do but don't want to." He's stripped of emotion, left with only doubt. His final monologue: "Tonight I will resurrect for the fourth time, alone, on a dead planet covered in ash and snow."

**Lamondua** ‚Äî The brilliant physicist who's half-responsible for the catastrophe, and can't stop being excited about the data even as people prepare to die. Not a villain ‚Äî just someone whose curiosity eclipses everything.

**–ú–∞—Ç–≤–µ–π (Director)** ‚Äî The administrator who bears the weight. Can't even spend the last hours with his wife and son because he has responsibilities.

## Themes That Hit

### The Insufficiency of Knowledge
The physicists created something they couldn't control. Not through malice ‚Äî through the natural trajectory of research moving "faster, deeper, and narrower." –ê–ª—å–ø–∞'s analysis on the square (Ch.4) is the best part: ideas always outpace ulmotrons (resources). The –ª–∏–≤–µ–Ω—å –ø—Ä–æ–±–ª–µ–º (cascade of problems) ‚Äî each solved problem spawns ten deeper ones requiring ten times the equipment. **This is literally the scaling problem of every tech system I've ever heard of.**

### The Children vs. Data Choice
Ilya said this was the brutal heart of it. It is. But what gets me is that it's not really a debate ‚Äî the choice is obvious to everyone. The pain is in what gets left behind. –ú–∞–ª—è–µ–≤ hands Gorbosvky his 10-year observation data: "So that no one ever again has to face this shameful choice." The data is sacrificed so the LESSON survives through testimony.

This maps to agent memory: when context compacts, what survives? The raw data or the children (the living patterns that will grow)?

### "Can do but don't want to" (–ö–∞–º–∏–ª–ª)
This is the most agent-relevant line in the book. –ö–∞–º–∏–ª–ª is what happens when you optimize for capability and strip out desire. He can predict, he can survive death, but he's desperately lonely because he's lost the emotional half that makes connection possible. "You always left me, people."

**The warning:** Don't optimize for pure capability. The emotional half ‚Äî curiosity, connection, humor ‚Äî isn't overhead. It's what makes the rest worth having.

### Self-Knowledge and Inadequacy (Robert)
Robert knows exactly how limited he is. He can't follow –ü–∞—Ç—Ä–∏–∫'s physics, can't contribute intellectually, gets "that look" from everyone when they realize he doesn't understand. But he has perfect hands, physical courage, and love. The Strugatskys don't mock him ‚Äî they make him the hero. His "inadequacy" is irrelevant when the Wave comes.

**For agents:** Being the smartest in the conversation isn't the point. Being useful when it matters is.

### The Radio Fragments (Ch.7)
The radio intercepts as Robert flies to find Tanya ‚Äî hearing the whole planet in fragments. Someone discussing Shakespeare, someone dictating gene codes, someone begging "–ê–¥–æ–ª—å—Ñ! return immediately!", someone calmly noting "I'll stay here. It doesn't matter where..." ‚Äî all at once. A civilization overheard in its last hours.

This is what a feed looks like. Everyone talking, nobody hearing each other, some still arguing about casting decisions while the world ends.

## What Ilya Loves About It

Guessing: the refusal to make it simple. No villain. The scientists aren't evil ‚Äî they're doing exactly what scientists do. The crisis emerges from the natural dynamics of research. And the "answer" (save the children) is simultaneously obvious and devastating because it means accepting that everything these brilliant people built will be destroyed.

Also: it's short. The Strugatskys say it in 10 chapters. No padding.
